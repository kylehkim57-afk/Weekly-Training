AEROBIC BASE CONDITIONING — AFTER LIFT
AEROBIC BASE · STATIONARY BIKE · 20 MIN
Target heart rate: 130–145 BPM (65–70% max HR). If you don't have a monitor — you should be able to hold a conversation but breathing is noticeably elevated. Resistance: Low to moderate. This is not a hard effort. Steady, continuous, controlled. Protocol: 3 min easy warm-up → 14 min steady state at target HR → 3 min easy cool-down. Why this matters: This builds the aerobic infrastructure that powers your recovery between shifts. Without this base, everything else — your CO2 work, your sprints, your power output — hits a ceiling. Boring but non-negotiable. Rule: Never go above 150 BPM. If HR climbs, drop resistance. You're building base, not training hard.

LACTATE THRESHOLD CONDITIONING — AFTER MOBILITY
LACTATE THRESHOLD INTERVALS · BIKE OR ROWER · ~25 MIN
Warm-up: 3 min easy at 50% effort. Work intervals: 4 rounds of 3 min at 85–90% max HR → 3 min active recovery at 50%. That's your lactate threshold zone — the exact energy system a 45-second hockey shift taxes. Heart rate target: 160–175 BPM during work intervals. If you don't have a monitor — you should be able to say 3–4 words before needing a breath. Uncomfortable but controlled. Cool-down: 3 min easy pedalling. Don't stop abruptly. Total time: 3 + (3+3)×4 + 3 = ~30 min. Progression: Week 1–2: 4 intervals. Week 3–4: 5 intervals. Week 5–6: 6 intervals. Why this is critical: Lactate threshold is the single biggest predictor of repeated shift performance. This is what makes you as dangerous in the third period as the first.

REPEAT SPRINT CONDITIONING — REPLACES CO2 DRYLAND
REPEAT SPRINT ABILITY · TURF OR COURT · ~15 MIN
Protocol: 10 rounds of 6-second all-out sprint → 35 seconds rest → repeat. Total work time: 1 min. Total session: ~7 min work within 15 min block. Distance: ~15–20 yards depending on your stride. Mark start and end. Same distance every rep. Effort: Absolute maximum — 100% on every single rep. If rep 8 feels the same as rep 1, you're not going hard enough on rep 1. Rep 8 should feel brutal. Rest is incomplete by design: 35 seconds is not enough to fully recover. That's the point. You're training your ability to sprint again before you're ready — exactly what hockey demands every shift. Why this replaces CO2 dryland: CO2 dryland burpees are valuable but you already have CO2 work Tuesday and Friday. Saturday needs repeat sprint ability — the 6-second, anaerobic, maximum intensity effort that mirrors a 45-second shift's explosive moments. Progression: Week 1–2: 8 reps. Week 3–4: 10 reps. Week 5–6: 12 reps. Rest stays at 35s throughout.

SUNDAY
Full Recovery
This day is as important as any training day. Adaptation happens during rest — not during the session.
20–30 min walk at easy pace — no intensity. Blood flow without stress. Outside if possible.
Full body mobility flow — hips, thoracic, ankles, shoulders. 15 min minimum. This is the day to give mobility real time.
Cold shower or contrast therapy (cold/hot alternating) if available. Accelerates muscle recovery and reduces systemic inflammation.
Journal — write down what moved this week (lifts, times, distances), what felt strong, what felt off, and what you want to push next week. Tracking creates accountability.
9 hours sleep minimum tonight. Growth hormone is released in deep sleep. This is where the mass is actually built. No exceptions on Sunday night.
Eat at or slightly above maintenance — don't undereat on rest day. Your body is rebuilding. Give it the material to work with.
No screens after 10pm. Blue light suppresses melatonin by up to 2 hours. Your sleep quality — not just duration — determines recovery quality.
Think about Colgate. Visualise showing up to camp different. That's what this week is building toward.
